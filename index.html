Research Question

Pakistan has hit "record lows"
— and always bounced back.

The Global Polio Eradication Initiative has eliminated wild poliovirus in 123 countries since 1988. Yet Pakistan cycles through near-success and relapse — not randomly, but driven by three structural forces: cross-border viral corridors, unreachable geographic pockets, and dangerously misleading official data.

Q1 — Global Trajectory

Pakistan vs. the world:
A race that hasn't ended.

The world reduced polio cases per million by over 99.9% since 1988. Pakistan broadly follows — but exhibits repeated surges that keep it among the last two endemic countries on Earth.

Paralytic Polio Cases per Million — Regional Comparison (1980–2023)

Pakistan's per-capita burden now exceeds Afghanistan's in recent years — a structural failure, not just a security one. India eliminated polio in 2014; Nigeria in 2020. Both had comparable or lower coverage at the time.

Q2 — The Border Effect

The border isn't a barrier.
It's a highway.

WHO and Nature Communications (2025) confirmed genetically identical WPV1 strains on both sides of the Pakistan–Afghanistan border — proving active cross-border viral flow. Where do Afghan refugees concentrate in Pakistan? Exactly where polio cases cluster.

Q3 — The Coverage Gap

The government said 99%.
Reality said 52%.

Three sources measure Pakistan's polio vaccination coverage — and they tell radically different stories. ADMIN (facility-reported) data consistently overstates the WHO/UNICEF independent estimate (WUENIC) by up to 48 percentage points.

01

Three measures, three stories

Every year, three numbers are recorded for Pakistan's OPV3 coverage: ADMIN (what facilities report), OFFICIAL (government's own estimate), and WUENIC (WHO/UNICEF independent audit). They should agree. They don't.

02

The dangerous peak (2010)

In 2010, ADMIN reported 99.56% coverage — near-perfect. WUENIC estimated just 52%. A 48-percentage-point gap. During this very window of false confidence, cases were climbing toward the catastrophic 2014 peak of 328.

03

Coverage vs. cases (the correlation)

Case spikes consistently follow periods of low WUENIC coverage (r = −0.30). When WUENIC dropped to 52% (2009–10), cases climbed. Recovery to 75%+ by 2017 → historic low of 8 cases the same year.

04

The 2019 paradox

But the 2019 surge happened at 84% WUENIC coverage — well above threshold. This signals a fundamental shift: the problem is no longer system-wide coverage collapse, but localised, geographically concentrated unreachable pockets in border districts.

Global Ranking

86% coverage. Rank 87th.
Still not enough.

Pakistan ranked 87th out of 218 countries in 2023 OPV3 coverage at 86% — above the 80% WHO target. India and Nigeria eliminated polio with comparable or lower coverage. The difference: they closed the geographic gaps.
